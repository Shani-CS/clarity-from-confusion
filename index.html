Clarity from Confusion

How the visualization community draws machine-learning error & accuracy — and why nobody draws it the same way twice.

Based on the survey by Shani Spivak, Mackenzie Creamer, Melanie Tory & Michelle A. Borkin — Khoury College of Computer Sciences, Northeastern University. A reading of 68 papers across IEEE TVCG, ACM CHI, and ACM IUI (2019–2023).

01 — The atom of error

It all reduces to four cells.

Almost every performance number a model reports is built from the same quartet of outcomes — the contents of a confusion matrix. Tap a cell to see what it means and why the difference between them is rarely symmetric.

TPTrue Positive
FPFalse Positive
FNFalse Negative
TNTrue Negative
True Positive

The model says positive, and it's right. The hits — the cases we hoped to catch and did.

▸ click any cell — these four are the only values that vary independently

Not all of these errors weigh the same. In cancer screening, a false negative can mean a missed diagnosis and lost treatment time; a false positive causes stress and extra tests, but usually resolves once the mistake surfaces. The matrix is the one common view that keeps those two failure modes visibly distinct.

02 — Same ingredients, different recipes

Every metric is those four cells, rearranged.

The most-reported metrics in the survey aren't independent — they're different ratios of TP, FP, FN and TN. Which is exactly why two papers can describe "performance" and never be comparing the same thing.

Accuracy

(TP+TN) / total
Share of all predictions that are correct. Says nothing about which errors happened.
TPTNFPFN

Precision

TP / (TP+FP)
Of everything flagged positive, how much really was. Punishes false alarms.
TPFP

Recall / sensitivity

TP / (TP+FN)
Of everything that truly was positive, how much we caught. Punishes misses.
TPFN

Specificity

TN / (FP+TN)
Of everything truly negative, how much we correctly left alone.
TNFP

F1 Score

2TP / (2TP+FP+FN)
Harmonic mean of precision & recall. Its non-linear pull on TP lets you weight costly mistakes.
TPFPFN

ROC / AUC

TPR vs FPR
Trades sensitivity against 1−specificity across thresholds. Area under the curve = how well classes separate.
TPFNFPTN
03 — What the field actually reaches for

Across 490 charts, simple wins.

The authors coded every error-and-accuracy figure in all 68 papers. The result is a clear hierarchy — and a quiet irony hiding inside it.

Step 01 / Frequency
108

Tables lead — by a lot.

The single most common way to communicate model performance isn't a chart at all. It's a table. Plain numbers, dropped into a grid.

Step 02 / Frequency

Then comes plain text.

Second place goes to performance reported inside the prose of the paper. Visual encoding often simply isn't the priority — which makes cross-paper comparison harder.

Step 03 / Frequency

Line & bar charts: the familiar workhorses.

When authors do reach for a chart, they reach for the ones everyone already reads — lines for accuracy over epochs, bars for model-to-model comparison.

The richest view is the rarest of the common five.

Heatmaps — almost always confusion matrices — are the only view that keeps false positives and false negatives distinct. Yet they trail the simpler formats. The most informative encoding is also the least familiar one.

04 — Which metric carries the story

Two metrics do most of the talking.

Of every metric presented across the corpus, accuracy and the confusion-matrix metrics together account for more than half — but accuracy, the one that hides which error occurred, leads.

Accuracy — 35.3%
The plurality. Correct ÷ total, blind to which error happened.
Confusion metrics — 19.5%
Second most common. The only family that breaks errors apart.
Everything else — 45.2%
Precision, recall, F1, AUC, and dozens of one-off measures.
05 — A field still finding its footing

Performance figures surged — then dipped.

Error-and-accuracy visualizations climbed steadily and peaked in 2022 before falling off the following year. And the long tail keeps growing: of 25 distinct chart types observed, a third appeared in only a single year.

08 — How the marks are made

Two perceptual channels do the heavy lifting.

Coding each figure against Munzner's channel rankings, the authors found authors gravitate toward a small set of encodings — position and spatial region above all.

Dominant visual channels (of 296 encodings)
Position
197
Spatial region

Position ∩ region

Position on a common scale — the most accurately read channel there is — anchors most charts. Spatial region powers the heatmap. Where they combine, you get the confusion matrix: efficient for many classes, but trading away that high-accuracy position channel to do it.

09 — The call

Keep the creativity. Build in the comparability.

The survey isn't a critique of inventive design — it's a plea to pair it with shared conventions, so stakeholders can actually weigh one model's risks against another's. As models reach higher-stakes domains, abstracting error types away for a single tidy accuracy number carries real human cost.
